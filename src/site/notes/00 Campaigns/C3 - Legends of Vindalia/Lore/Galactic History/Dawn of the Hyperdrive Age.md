<h2> The Hyperdrive Age </h2>

It has been roughly six to eight decades since the first functional hyperdrive vessels crossed the void between stars. In that time, interstellar travel has moved from experimental to a fragile but growing network of routes linking distant worlds. Despite this progress, the galaxy remains far from fully connected. The majority of space is still uncharted, and travel between systems continues to demand patience, skill, and caution.

Shipyards on developed worlds are capable of producing hyperdrive-capable vessels, and many governments, trading enterprises, and exploratory guilds operate fleets that regularly traverse the known routes. Safe travel through hyperspace depends on carefully mapped coordinates that avoid stars, rogue planets, gravity wells, and other hazards that can tear a ship apart if encountered at hyperspace velocities. These safe corridors are the result of decades of surveying, failed expeditions, and painstaking charting.

Only a handful of reliable hyperspace routes exist at present. These early corridors link small clusters of systems where repeated travel has confirmed relatively safe passage. Even along these routes, ships rarely make a single uninterrupted jump between stars. Instead, navigators commonly plot a sequence of shorter jumps, recalculating coordinates between each transition to ensure safety.

Because of these limitations, interstellar journeys remain long undertakings. Travel between nearby systems along established routes may take several weeks, while voyages to worlds off the main corridors can take months. Inexperienced pilots often opting for sub-light speeds, simply out of caution. For remote settlements, the arrival of an interstellar vessel may occur only once or twice a year. When a trader does arrive, their cargo often represents an entire season’s worth of goods, news, and technological exchange.

Outside the known corridors, navigation becomes significantly more dangerous. Charting a new route requires survey ships to advance in careful increments, recording gravitational hazards and transmitting navigation data back to sponsoring organizations. Many such expeditions never return. Hyperspace accidents are uncommon along well-known routes but remain a constant threat in unexplored regions. Stories of ships emerging damaged, lost in unknown systems, or never emerging at all continue to circulate among spacers.

The limited number of safe routes has also created strategic choke points. Traffic between certain regions must pass through narrow corridors of mapped space, concentrating trade and travel into predictable pathways. These routes have become valuable assets for governments, corporations, and criminal groups alike. Pirates and raiders frequently lurk along these corridors, targeting vessels that cannot easily deviate from their plotted course.

### Interstellar Communication

Communication between star systems has developed alongside hyperdrive travel, but it remains limited by the same challenges that affect navigation and exploration. While most settled worlds maintain robust planetary communications networks, transmitting messages across interstellar distances is far less reliable.

The earliest method of interstellar communication was simple physical transport. Messages, navigation data, and official dispatches were carried aboard courier vessels traveling established hyperspace routes. Even today, this remains the most dependable way to transmit large amounts of information between systems. Many traders and survey ships routinely carry sealed data packets alongside their cargo.

More recently, specialized long-range relay transmitters have begun to appear along the most heavily travelled hyperspace corridors. These installations allow for compressed signal bursts to be transmitted between relay stations, gradually passing messages from system to system. While faster than physical couriers, these relay chains are still limited in reach and capacity, and most networks only extend along the safest and most frequently traveled routes.

Because of these constraints, interstellar communication remains slow and fragmented. Messages between nearby systems along established corridors may take days or weeks to arrive, while communication with frontier worlds can still take months. News spreads unevenly across the stars, and distant regions often operate with little awareness of events unfolding elsewhere in the galaxy.
