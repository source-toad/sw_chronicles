### Overview

Candoria is a temperate forest world in the **Mid Rim**, situated along one of the region’s **early long-distance trade corridors** connecting a handful of developing star systems toward the Coreward regions. While it has never been a major hub, occasional travellers, merchants, and scouts pass through these routes, often noting the planet’s unusual combination of dense forests, upland highlands, and hints of ancient occupation.  

The planet’s continents are dominated by **vast, multilayered forests** that stretch for thousands of kilometres. Towering trees form dense canopies that block much of the sunlight from reaching the forest floor, creating dim and cool conditions beneath. The forests themselves are largely unbroken, interrupted only by **river valleys, exposed ridges, and the gradual ascent toward mountain ranges**, where the terrain opens into broader **highland belts**.

These highlands - rolling upland slopes, elevated valleys, and occasional mesas - provide the **only significant open ground suitable for settlement, travel, or agriculture**. Both ancient and modern communities have consistently taken advantage of these elevated regions. They offer natural protection from the most dangerous forest predators and provide clear lines of sight across the surrounding wilderness.

Candoria’s rivers, mountains, and highland belts have made settlement and exploration possible, but much of the planet remains **largely untamed**. The forests continue to encroach on clearings, and vast interior regions are still unknown to those who traverse the early trade corridors. For those who spend time on Candoria, the world feels ancient, and dangerous, but simultaneously peaceful. While the system itself is filled with people, the difficulty of mass urbanisation of Candoria maintains it's status as a frontier world, free from the hustle and bustle of some of the other planets in the system.

### Planetary Environment

Candoria’s continents are covered by **extensive temperate forests**, many of which appear extremely old by planetary standards. Tree growth forms layered canopies that block much of the sunlight from reaching the forest floor, creating cool, dim conditions beneath the upper branches.

Key environmental characteristics include:

- Multi-layered forest canopies reaching hundreds of metres above the ground
- Thick root systems and heavy undergrowth that make ground travel slow
- Large river networks cutting through deep valleys and serving as the most reliable travel routes
- Regular fog banks and rainfall trapped beneath the canopy

Wildlife on Candoria tends toward large herbivores adapted to the dense vegetation, along with predators that rely more on sound and scent than sight within the dim forest interior.

### The Highland Ranges

Across Candoria’s continents, long **mountain ranges rise gradually from the surrounding forests**, forming wide belts of elevated highland terrain before the peaks themselves begin. Rather than abrupt mesas or isolated plateaus, these regions are characterised by **broad upland slopes, broken ridges, and open highland valleys** that stretch for hundreds of kilometres along the mountain fronts.

As the forests climb toward these uplands, the ecosystem begins to change.

These highland regions share several characteristics:

- The dense lowland forests thin into **shorter woodland and open highland clearings**
- Rolling upland ground provides **wide, stable surfaces suitable for settlement**
- Rocky ridges and foothills create **natural defensive terrain**
- Higher elevations offer **clearer views across the surrounding forests**

For travellers and settlers, these highlands provide the rarest resource on Candoria: open ground that does not need to be constantly reclaimed from the forest.

Because of this, both ancient and modern settlements have tended to form along these elevated belts where the forests begin to give way to highland terrain.

The uplands also provide a practical advantage. Many of Candoria’s most dangerous predators and large fauna dwell within the deep forest environments below. Settlements established along the highland slopes gain a measure of natural protection, as the thinner vegetation and exposed terrain make it more difficult for such creatures to approach unseen.

Scattered across several of these highland regions are **weathered remains of older stone structures**, suggesting that earlier inhabitants may have recognised the same advantages long before the current age of settlement.

Across Candoria’s continents are scattered **ancient stone plateaus**, uplifted bedrock formations that rise sharply above the surrounding forest.

These highlands share several characteristics:
- Wide, level surfaces suitable for habitation
- Reduced forest density compared to the lowland canopy
- Natural cliff faces and defensible approaches
- Clearer views across the surrounding terrain

For travellers and settlers, these plateaus offer the only places where construction and agriculture can occur without the constant pressure of the surrounding forests.

Because of this, most known settlements—past and present—are located on these elevated formations.

### Ancient Forest Ruins

Hidden deep within Candoria’s forests are scattered **remnants of ancient structures**, long since reclaimed by the planet’s relentless vegetation.

In places where the canopy briefly opens—along riverbanks, landslides, or exposed ridges—travellers occasionally uncover fragments of what appear to be **old stone constructions** buried beneath layers of soil, moss, and root growth.

Known features include:

- Broken wall lines barely visible beneath thick root systems
- Carved stone blocks protruding from eroded slopes
- Sections of paved ground revealed where heavy rains have stripped away soil
- Large foundation outlines detectable only when viewed from elevated terrain

In many cases the structures are not immediately recognisable as ruins. What appear to be natural rock formations sometimes reveal **straight edges or fitted joints** once the surrounding vegetation is cleared away.

The forests covering these sites are immense and ancient. Towering trees and deep soil layers suggest that the landscape has been left undisturbed for an extremely long time. In many areas, roots have grown directly through stonework, splitting walls apart and burying entire structures beneath the forest floor.

Because of this, most discoveries occur by accident—exposed by storms, river erosion, or the slow shifting of the terrain.

What these structures once formed is unclear. Some appear to be isolated buildings, while others hint at **larger layouts hidden beneath the forest canopy**, suggesting that parts of Candoria’s wilderness may once have been settled or cultivated.

No surviving records describe these settlements, and it remains uncertain **how long ago the forests reclaimed the land**. Some scholars believe the ruins could predate the earliest known exploration of the region by many thousands of years, while others suggest the forests may have expanded slowly over time, gradually swallowing what once stood beneath them.

### Early Exploration

Candoria entered wider galactic awareness during the era when neighbouring star systems began establishing **regular long-distance trade corridors** between inhabited worlds. Merchant vessels, scouts, and exploratory expeditions charting these routes identified Candoria as a habitable world positioned along a useful—if secondary—corridor between several developing systems.

Initial reports described the planet as rich in biological life but **challenging for permanent settlement**.

Several conditions discouraged large-scale colonisation:

- The deep forests proved extremely difficult to clear and maintain over long periods
- Mineral resources appeared limited compared to many other worlds being settled at the time
- Thick vegetation and rugged terrain made large overland infrastructure impractical

Instead, the first lasting settlements appeared along **the highland belts surrounding Candoria’s mountain ranges**. These elevated regions offered clearer ground, thinner vegetation, and natural defensive terrain. Small communities established themselves there as **rest stops, trading enclaves, and observation settlements** serving travellers moving along the regional corridor.

Over time these highland settlements became the planet’s primary inhabited regions, while the vast lowland forests remained largely undisturbed.

### Present Activity (25,000 BBY)

In the present era, Candoria remains a **lightly settled frontier world**.

Population centres are small and widely scattered, usually situated within the highland regions where the forest begins to thin near the mountain ranges. These settlements tend to be practical and self-reliant, relying on nearby rivers, upland soil, and controlled forest clearings for sustenance.

Activity on the planet typically revolves around:
- Modest trade stations supporting vessels travelling the regional corridor
- Small agricultural or hunting communities established on the highland slopes
- Occasional expeditions into the surrounding forests for rare biological specimens

Beyond the inhabited uplands, much of Candoria’s surface remains only loosely explored. The immense forest canopy limits aerial visibility, and large portions of the lowlands have never been surveyed in detail.

From time to time, storms, landslides, or river erosion expose **unexpected fragments of ancient structures beneath the forest**, prompting short-lived expeditions before the jungle inevitably begins reclaiming the site again.

---

### Reputation

Among traders, scouts, and travellers moving along the corridor routes, Candoria has developed a quiet reputation as a world that **feels older than the civilizations that pass through it**.

It is commonly associated with:
- Endless forests stretching unbroken across entire continents
- Highland settlements perched along the edges of the mountain ranges
- Strange remnants of forgotten construction buried beneath ancient woodland

Those who spend time on Candoria often leave with the sense that the forests are not simply wilderness. Beneath the deep soil and towering canopy, fragments of earlier structures continue to surface—suggesting that parts of the planet may once have been far more open and inhabited than they appear today.

Whether the forests slowly reclaimed those lands over countless centuries, or whether something caused them to spread across the world long ago, remains unknown.
